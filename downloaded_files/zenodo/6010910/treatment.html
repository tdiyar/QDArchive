Addenda to the holotype original description based on specimens from Venezuela.

Male terminalia (Figs. 38–44): Black. Medioapical region of hypandrium with concavity (Fig. 39). Epandrium wide basally with finger-like lobe placed dorsally. Apex of finger-like lobe rounded in lateral view (Fig. 40); subepandrial sclerite without lateral projections, apical margin rounded (Fig. 41); gonocoxite with apical 1/3 digitiform (Fig. 42); gonostyle subrectangular, covered with small setae anteriorly and with long setae posteriorly (Figs. 42, 43); aedeagus with two long prongs (Fig. 44); ejaculatory apodeme wide in lateral view (Fig. 44). Female terminalia (Fig. 46, 47): Tergite VIII expanded laterally (Figs. 46, 47); tergite IX+X slightly membranous medially (Fig. 46); apex of sternite VIII truncate with mediaoapical slit (Fig. 47); hypoproct with spiniform setae; median sclerite at posterior end of furca (remnant of tergite IX) absent; two oval sclerotized spermathecal capsules.

Comments. Ctenodontina baleta (Walker) comb. nov., Pachychoeta caracasae Martin syn. nov. and Pachychoeta inca Martin syn. nov. were allocated in Lecania Macquart. However, these species have diagnostic characters of Ctenodontina. Lecania is a paraphyletic group and has no diagnostic characters defined. According to Artigas & Papavero (1995), Lecania “is an extremely varied assembly of species. This group needs a thorough revision to decide whether there are several genera involved or only one. The variation of the several characters is amazing, and there is an incredible combination of them”.

Ctenodontina baleta (Walker) comb. nov., P. caracasae Martin syn. nov., P. inca Martin syn. nov. as well as C. nairae and C. martini, do not have a spinous swelling ventrally on male hind femora (Figs. 6, 7). However, as discussed by Fisher (1985) and Vieira (2012b), Ctenodontina is better defined by its very distinctive male and female terminalia and C. baleta (Walker) comb. nov., P. caracasae Martin syn. nov. and P. inca Martin syn. nov. by the following characters: female ovipositor with an apical comb of spines, and tergite VIII broadly expanded laterally, densely setose and cupped beneath, completely obscuring sternite VIII in profile and male specimens with an epandrium narrowed apically (usually terminating in a finger-like lobe), distinctly shorter than sternite VIII (Fisher & Hespenheide 1992; Fisher 2009; Vieira 2012b).
